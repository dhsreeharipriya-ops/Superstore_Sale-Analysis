This report presents a comprehensive analysis of the Superstore's global sales operations across 147 countries from 2011 to 2014. Using a dataset of 51,290 transactions and 25,035 unique orders, this project applies data analytics and business analytics techniques to diagnose a critical business challenge: despite strong and consistent revenue growth, the company's profit margin has stagnated at approximately 11.6%, and nearly one in four transactions is unprofitable.
Research questions : 
1.	Which product categories and sub-categories are the most and least profitable, and what drives the difference?
2.	How does the company's discounting strategy affect profitability, and is there a discount threshold beyond which orders consistently lose money?
3.	Which regions are underperforming relative to their sales volume, and what are the likely causes?
4.	Is the business growing sustainably, or is revenue growth masking a deteriorating profit structure?
Tools Used:
•	Microsoft Excel — data cleaning, feature engineering, pivot tables, and summary statistics.
•	Power BI — interactive dashboard with slicers for category, region, year, segment, and discount band.
Key Findings:
F1:	Discounting is the single largest driver of profit loss.
Orders with no discount earned a 25.32% profit margin and generated $1,770,695 in profit. Medium-discount orders (20–50% off) lost $402,100 at a –25.80% margin. High-discount orders (55–85% off) lost a further $412,582 at an extraordinary –111.02% margin — meaning the company paid more in discounts than it earned in revenue on those transactions. In total, medium and high discounts alone destroyed over $814,000 in profit value.
F2: Tables sub-category is structurally loss-making — but only because of discounts.
Tables generated a net loss of $64,083 on $757,034 in sales, a margin of –8.47%. However, when discount bands are isolated: Tables with no discount generated +$60,447 in profit (a healthy +23.1% margin). Tables sold at medium discount lost $67,515, and at high discount lost $65,026. This is a critical insight — Tables are not an inherently bad product. The loss is entirely caused by excessive discounting. The product itself, sold at full price, is profitable.
F3: Southeast Asia has the most severe regional margin compression.
Southeast Asia generated $884,438 in sales but only $17,852 in profit — a margin of just 2.02%. This is the lowest of all 13 regions. In comparison, North Asia generated $848,349 in sales with $165,578 in profit — a 19.52% margin on nearly identical revenue. The gap of 17.5 percentage points on similar sales volumes strongly suggests a regional discount or cost structure problem rather than a market demand issue. Medium discounts in Southeast Asia alone destroyed $52,903.
F4: Revenue is growing strongly, but profit margin has flatlined.
Revenue grew from $2.26M in 2011 to $4.30M in 2014 — a 90.3% increase over four years, with year-on-year growth rates of +18.5%, +27.2%, and +26.3%. However, profit margin has remained within a narrow 11.02%–11.99% band across all four years. This decoupling of revenue growth from margin improvement is a warning signal: it indicates that growth is being achieved partly by selling at increasingly unprofitable discount levels, not through genuine efficiency gains.
F5: Technology is the highest-value category; Furniture is the weakest.
Technology generated $4.74M in revenue and $663,779 in profit at a 13.99% margin. Office Supplies, while lower in revenue ($3.79M), achieved a comparable margin of 13.69% with far fewer large transactions and represents highly efficient, high-volume low-cost sales. Furniture generated the highest revenue of all three categories at $4.11M but delivered only $286,782 in profit at a 6.98% margin — roughly half the efficiency of Technology and Office Supplies. Furniture is also the category most affected by discount-driven losses.
